Multi-modal dataset for engagement intention prediction in human-robot interaction

Author: Vincent Pfaefflin
Affiliation: Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT)
Collaboration: Bosch Robotics Lab, Renningen, Germany

This dataset captures pre-engagement behavior in a robot-centric, multi-target environment, including both stationary and moving robot conditions, 360° fisheye video and 3D full-body poses.

Overview

This dataset supports research on engagement intention prediction in human-robot interaction. It focuses on the period before a person starts interacting with a robot, rather than only modeling ongoing interaction.

The data was collected in a controlled lab environment using a gamified puzzle task in which participants moved between multiple stations, one of which was a mobile robot. Because the robot was only one of several possible targets, the dataset captures realistic ambiguity between approaching the robot and approaching another station.

Why this dataset?

Existing HRI datasets often focus on detecting who is currently engaged, rather than predicting whether someone is about to engage. They also frequently rely on static robots, single-target setups, fixed external cameras or scripted behavior.

This dataset addresses these gaps by capturing natural, goal-driven behavior in a multi-target setting with robot mobility and fully robot-centric sensing.

Pre-engagement behavior

Captures the transitional phase before interaction begins, including movement, orientation and approach behavior.

Multi-target ambiguity

Participants may approach the robot or one of six alternative stations, making the prediction task more realistic.

Robot mobility

Includes both stationary and moving robot conditions, enabling evaluation in dynamic scenarios.

Natural task context

Interaction emerges from a gamified puzzle task rather than explicit instructions to engage with the robot.

Experimental setup

The interaction area covers roughly 7 m × 7 m. It contains six tables arranged in an elliptical formation around a robot located at the center, which functions as the seventh station. The distance between the robot and the tables ranges from approximately 3.1 m to 4.5 m.

The setup was designed to allow sufficient space for navigation while preserving a clear spatial relationship between the robot and the surrounding stations.

Task and conditions

Each of the seven stations contains a unique colored puzzle with one visible center piece and six outer pieces distributed across the other stations. At the beginning, each station contains one upward-facing piece identifying the puzzle for that location, and six random face-down pieces belonging to the other puzzles. Pieces belonging to the robot station include an additional "R" marking.

Participants solve the task by picking up one face-down puzzle piece at a time, revealing its color, carrying it to the matching station, placing it and then picking up a new piece from that station. This process repeats until all puzzles are completed.

Stationary condition

The robot remains at the central position and acts as a fixed interaction target.

Moving condition

The robot moves along a predefined path between two locations. The experimenter manually pauses and resumes its motion to allow puzzle interaction on the robot.

Participant instructions

To reduce behavioral bias, the study was presented to participants as an investigation of pose detection from on-board fisheye cameras. Participants started from a designated starting point after a verbal “Go” signal and completed the task at their own pace without a time limit.

The following rules were given:

  • Only one puzzle piece may be carried at a time.
  • After picking up a piece, the participant must locate the matching station and insert it into the corresponding puzzle.

Sensors and data modalities

All data is recorded from on-board robot sensors. Two fisheye cameras mounted on a vertical pole provide a full 360° robot-centric view around the robot.

Annotation pipeline

The dataset was annotated using a semi-automatic event-based pipeline. During data collection, the experimenter triggered timestamped events whenever a participant picked up a puzzle piece or reached the destination station.

Step 1: Live annotation

Pickup events mark the start of an engagement intention trajectory, while drop events mark its end.

Step 2: Manual review

All event labels were reviewed and corrected post hoc to improve timing accuracy and consistency.

Step 3: Automatic labeling

For each drop event, the participant’s position was compared to the robot and station locations to determine the interaction target.

Label semantics

  • Pickup → Drop: engagement intention trajectory toward a target
  • After Drop → next Pickup: engaged with the reached target

Labels and benchmark formulation

The original labels identify whether a participant is intending to engage with the robot or with one of the six stations.

Label type Description
Intending to engage with robot Trajectory ends at the robot station
Intending to engage with station X Trajectory ends at one of the six table stations
Engaged with robot / station Period after arrival and before the next pickup

For the binary benchmark task, trajectories toward the robot are treated as positive and trajectories toward the stations as negative.

Participants

The dataset contains 12 sessions, each associated with a unique participant. Participants were students and researchers working on various robotics topics, but they were not familiar with the actual purpose of the experiment.

Data collection took place at the Bosch Robotics Lab in Renningen, Germany.

Data processing and augmentation

For engagement intention prediction, only the pre-engagement phase is used. Raw trajectories consist of 3D full-body poses and the corresponding target location over time. Periods labeled as engaged are excluded from the benchmark task.

Fixed-length tracklets are extracted using a sliding-window approach. To reduce dependence on absolute target position, each tracklet is translated into the coordinate frame of the target at the final frame, such that the target is mapped to the origin.

By treating the stations as additional targets, each recording session yields many more positive and negative examples than when considering only the robot perspective. Additional random rotational augmentation is applied to improve class balance and generalization.

Benchmark tasks

  • Binary engagement intention prediction
  • Interaction target prediction
  • Temporal sequence modeling from short pose tracklets
  • Early intention prediction in static and dynamic robot conditions
